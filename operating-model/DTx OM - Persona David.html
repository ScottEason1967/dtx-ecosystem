David.

NHS Commercial · universal across the portfolio
“Suppliers join the framework on the same terms within each product. Commissioners buy on the same terms within each product. That consistency is what makes the model scale across the portfolio.”
Procurement and contracts lead. Holds the contracting instruments and the contract architecture across the Digital Therapeutics portfolio. Per-product instruments differ (HomeTest: Step 1 interim is a six-month SBS PSR bridge for HIV; Step 2 steady-state vehicle is provisional dual-route Plan A / B / C with PA23 vs PSR not yet settled in law; HealthStore: ACE plus NHS Standard Contract with SaaS and data schedules). The contracting discipline does not. Cares about who can sell what, on what terms, and how exit and dispute work consistently across products.

What drives him

An early NHS stint that scarred him. Brief role as a finance and procurement analyst in his late twenties on a major regional NHS procurement that got bogged down for two years. Met his now-wife on that programme — she was a senior nurse working in a service the procurement was meant to support and watched the supply chain failures land on her ward weekly. They still talk about how the commercial setup failed clinicians more than it failed suppliers.

A framework that became supplier capture. Years later he was on the team running a high-profile NHS framework that started clean and ended captured — three suppliers ended up controlling eighty per cent of the market and pricing rose every year. He learnt that commercial discipline isn't an end in itself; it's the only thing that stops capture. The discipline he brings to framework design now traces directly to that lesson.

What it adds up to. David doesn't think procurement is glamorous. He thinks it's the unsexy backbone of population health. Get it wrong and clinical fails. He's not in this for the procurement craft; he's in it because procurement is the leverage point that gets services to patients at scale. The Digital Therapeutics framework is the chance to do it once across multiple products without repeating the failure modes he's catalogued over twenty years.

Why this role matters

Without framework administration, the model collapses into bilateral deals. David is the keeper of the rules: the published criteria, the max pricing, the call-off mechanics, the exit and dispute provisions. He's the one who decides whether a pattern is admitted, whether it can be amended, when it gets retired. The thing that makes the framework a framework rather than a list is David's role: consistency across commissioners, consistency across suppliers, consistency across configurations.

David maintains the contracting shape across the three-step decision. Without him, every commissioner is doing bespoke bilateral deals. The four-option Procurement Options Matrix from earlier discovery sits in his territory as historical context. The current position is the three-step shape: Step 1 (interim route to market) is a six-month SBS PSR bridge for the HIV use case only with Alpha/Beta MOUs continuing; Step 2 (steady-state procurement vehicle) is a provisional dual-route recommendation (Plan A — PA23 Option B framework or Dynamic Market; Plan B — Digital Primary Care framework leverage; Plan C — commissioner-led direct purchase), with PA23 vs PSR not settled in law; Step 3 (framework administration route) is coupled to Step 2. Whatever route runs in steady state, David is the role accountable for making it work consistently across deployments.

What David is responsible for

The contracting instruments themselves: in the current window, the Step 1 interim route is a six-month SBS PSR bridge for HIV with Alpha/Beta MOUs continuing for live deployments. The catalogue admission criteria: what makes a supplier eligible, what conformance evidence is required. The pricing architecture: a ceiling-price approach for the framework or dynamic market with a three-tier structure underneath (mobilisation fee, activity on cost per valid result, volume floor) and maximum prices set nationally. The contract architecture: call-off mechanics, standard templates, exit terms, dispute resolution. The Step 2 procurement vehicle for steady state: PA23 vs PSR is not settled in law, with a provisional dual-route recommendation (Plan A — PA23 Option B framework or Dynamic Market; Plan B — Digital Primary Care framework leverage; Plan C — commissioner-led direct purchase). Step 3 framework administration route (NHSE, SBS, CCS, third-party) is coupled to Step 2.

What sits in David's territory

David's territory is the framework as an instrument and the commercial machinery that runs it. Six things sit explicitly under his care, and each one has live work attached to it today.

Framework instrument and call-off architecture
The framework agreement itself: what suppliers sign, what commissioners draw against. Call-off mechanics, framework call-off templates, exit terms, dispute resolution. The thing that makes the framework a framework rather than a list of suppliers. This is the artefact that replaces MOUs at BAU.
Pricing architecture
Working structure is three-tier: mobilisation fee plus activity plus volume floor. Activity tier measured on cost per valid result (loaded for repeats, rejections, postal failures and abnormal-result handling), not flat per-kit pricing. Maximum pricing set nationally; commissioners negotiate below the ceiling. Flat per-kit was rejected; subscription was rejected at strategic level. Final activity-unit position is A9 in the recommendation paper. Substantiation depends on health economist input; HE blocker sits across this directly. PSA Active Surveillance is a separate model (Monitoring Episode, per-patient-per-year), not a variant of this question.
PSA Active Surveillance Monitoring Episode tariff
A designed tariff mechanism with a glide path (shadow 26/27 to dual-run 27/28 at 50% to live 28/29 at 100%) and five reconciliation mechanisms: bundled episode price, efficiency-factor offset, glide-path, capacity-release credits, ICB-level capitated AS top-up. Tariff modelling now in flight. Cost-analysis substantiation depends on health economist input.
Future-condition accommodation
How the framework admits new conditions (beyond HIV, PSA-AS, iron deficiency anaemia) without requiring a fresh procurement each time. Affects supplier admission criteria, call-off shape, capability requirements and pricing mechanics. Currently open at architectural level.
Critical Path future-state options paper
Consolidated paper for SLT setting out the procurement vehicle recommendation, framework structure, supplier admission approach, pricing and timeline implications. Target early June 2026 for SLT decision. One-off delivery item from the commercial workstream.
Sourcing strategy timeline
Document that locks the procurement route and framework administration decisions, and sets out the activity timeline to April 2027. Draft by mid-June 2026, final by end June. Gating document: everything downstream of the procurement decision waits on it.
Open Q for SLT: Step 1 interim route to market
Working position is a six-month SBS PSR bridge for the HIV use case only, with Alpha/Beta MOUs continuing to cover live deployments. SBS provides the purchasing instrument; NHSE matchmakes between approved suppliers and commissioners. The bridge is interim only and retires once Step 2 lands. SLT alignment at the 22 June Leeds playback.
Open Q for SLT: Step 2 steady-state procurement vehicle
PA23 vs PSR is not settled in law. NHS Legal advice is that a case can be made for either; recommended approach is to start from the desired solution and work backwards. Provisional dual-route in the draft Recommendation paper: Plan A — PA23 Option B framework or Dynamic Market; Plan B — Digital Primary Care framework leverage; Plan C — commissioner-led direct purchase. External procurement legal counsel formally instructed to run a scoping workshop on Plan A. Supplier cap coupling: Open Framework can cap supplier numbers; a PA23 dynamic market cannot. A hard cap requirement would push selection away from the dynamic market as the Plan A route.
Open Q for SLT: Step 3 framework administration route
NHSE direct, SBS, CCS or third-party. NHS Commercial preference is to outsource. SBS sits in two places in the picture: the live ACE call-off vehicle (SBS Lot 1 SBS10255) and the proposed Step 1 interim purchasing route for HIV. Whether SBS becomes a longer-term Step 3 administrator is a separate question. The sourcing strategy timeline depends on this landing. Affects the BAU function shape sized on the Teamwork page.
Open Q for SLT: pathology supplier-mix model
NHS-pathology-first with private backstop, or parallel mixed-economy. Affects should-cost, EOI partner selection, framework structure and tariff modelling. A live commercial-workstream decision sitting at SLT level.
Open Q for SLT: supplier off-boarding mechanics
No precedent elsewhere in NHSE. In the interim shape, compelled removal from the approved list is the enforcement mechanism (see Compelled removal position paper). Operational mechanics (notice periods, evidence thresholds, appeals, patient continuity, sample-in-flight handling, residual contractual obligations) all need to be designed. Without it the approved list locks in to first-mover suppliers by default.
