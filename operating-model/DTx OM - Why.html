The reason this exists

Why.

The operating model exists so any Digital Therapeutics service can reach patients safely, work at scale, and deliver outcomes people can act on. NHSE orchestrates the market; suppliers, commissioners and providers do the work. Three workstreams sit inside one operating model: HomeTest (testing at home), HealthStore (prescribed digital therapeutics through the NHS App) and HealthSync (PHR-anchored consumer health data integrated into the NHS App). Three lenses explain why the model exists: the problem it solves, the economics that have to hold, and the reasons different audiences engage.

The spine of the model

Orchestrate the market. Do not supply it.

NHSE is the matchmaker, the market-maker, the bridge between innovation and care. It sets the standards, manages the framework, builds the integrations and runs the platform. It does not sign local contracts, own the billing, deliver care, or pick a single supplier. Anything that pulls NHSE into being the supplier breaks the 2030 vision.

Lens 01
Purpose
Why we're doing this. Four drivers, equally weighted: better access to care for people who can't easily reach a clinic; earlier action through screening, monitoring or therapy at scale; fairer outcomes by closing gaps in who gets reached and when; and system efficiency by easing pressure on primary care. Keyed to the DDTx 26/27 programme outcomes: volumes, equity, action taken.
Lens 02
Economics
The commercial logic. Suppliers must make money. Commissioners must save money or get more for the same money. The centre is funded sustainably through the orchestration role, not as a supplier. If any of these fails, the platform fails.
Lens 03
Adoption
Why each audience engages. Patient: privacy, convenience, dignity, access. Commissioner: easy buying (find it, decide, onboard, go live). Supplier: easy onboarding (onboard once, reuse across conditions). Clinician: pathway fit. NHSE: orchestration role at scale.
Where the economics actually resolves

The commissioner case lands at pathway level, not test level.

The economics lens above is necessary, not sufficient. In live commissioning, the cost of a new test and the saving it produces sit on different budget lines. The new test cost lands with one budget holder; the freed-up clinic capacity, the avoided onward referral and the workforce time recovered land with others. A commissioner already paying for cheaper venepuncture cannot fund the switch out of test economics alone — it reads as a cost pressure on top of an existing line.

The model only delivers at scale when the test sits inside a pathway and the pathway carries the economics — best-practice tariffs at pathway level, costed against the end-to-end condition pathway, not against the test as a component. Tariff articulation itself sits outside this operating model. The operating model's job is to make the dependency explicit: configurations only land where the commissioner has a viable funding route at the pathway level.

Cross-cutting questions that apply to every product, not just to one stage or one pathway. The three lenses above frame why the model exists. These three frame what is genuinely hard about making it work.

The shape of the model is not new

Same shape as existing NHS national models.

The model is not inventing a new way of doing things. It is taking proven NHS national mechanics and applying them to Digital Therapeutics. Accreditation sits with established bodies that already do it: CQC for provider quality, NICE EVA for digital therapeutics, the National Cancer Board, GIRFT or the relevant Royal College depending on the service. Listing works the way the National Choice Framework already lists eligible providers for elective care. Access works the way ERS already gives clinicians a national digital route to those providers. NHSE is the platform that brings the three together for Digital Therapeutics services; it is not the body that decides whether a service is clinically safe. That sits where it sits today. Same shape, new application.

Why each player engages

Three actors. One platform around them.

Patient, commissioner and supplier each have different reasons to engage across all three workstreams. Each product has its own patient archetype (Abdul on HomeTest, Pauline on HealthStore, Mei on HealthSync, with Errol's journey crossing all three) and its own supplier route (Joe for HomeTest kits, Marcus for HealthStore DTx, Tariq for HealthSync integration). They overlap with each other in specific pairs and share one centre: the service has to actually work, safely, and to be trusted. NHSE / Digital Therapeutics is the platform that holds it all together as orchestrator, not counterparty. Not a fourth player; the environment in which the three relate. Aisha, David and Ngozi are part of that platform: Aisha IS the clinical framework, David IS the commercial framework, Ngozi IS the information governance framework. The platform is the sum of those three layers, not a separate thing alongside them. The platform is product-agnostic; HomeTest, HealthStore and HealthSync each run on top of it.

The centre is the most important region.
The smallest overlap is the most powerful one. If the service doesn't actually work, if results aren't trusted, if it isn't clinically safe, none of the other regions matter. Everything else exists to make the centre true.
Health outcomes live between Patient & Commissioner.
This is the NHS mission expressed in pairs. Equity, access at scale, the population-level reasons commissioners spend money, the personal reasons patients use the service. Both sides of the same coin.
Economics live between Commissioner & Supplier.
Not in the patient's experience, but the foundation that lets it exist. Pricing, payment, risk, value for money. If the economics don't work for both sides, the service doesn't get bought, doesn't get built, doesn't reach the patient at all.
Aisha is part of the platform: the clinical framework.
Aisha doesn't sit inside any one circle. She is part of the NHSE/Digital Therapeutics envelope: the standards, the hazard log, the assurance bar that lets any pattern go live safely. Without her layer, the centre condition (the service works safely) cannot be true.
David is part of the platform: the commercial framework.
David doesn't sit inside any one circle either. He is part of the NHSE/Digital Therapeutics envelope: the procurement vehicle, the contract architecture, the commercial overlay that lets commissioners buy and suppliers sell at scale. The economics edge runs through his layer.
Ngozi is part of the platform: the information governance framework.
Ngozi doesn't sit inside any one circle either. She is part of the NHSE/Digital Therapeutics envelope: the DPIA, the DSA, the controller designation rules, the lawful basis position. Every data flow in the service crosses her framework. Without her layer, the service cannot lawfully process patient data at all.
The 2030 horizon

Digital Therapeutics as a standard part of healthcare pathways.

By March 2030, the model supports at least ten conditions with digital therapeutic data integrated into the patient record, including wearables. The NHS App is the front door; clinically-assured digital therapeutics are surfaced in-pathway, safe by design, equitably available; a national marketplace gives commissioners a single pane of glass; suppliers face one set of standards and one route to scale. NHSE remains the orchestrator, not the supplier. HomeTest, HealthStore and HealthSync progress at different rates within one operating model.
