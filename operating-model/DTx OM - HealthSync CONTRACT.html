HealthSync · CONTRACT

HealthSync at CONTRACT.

Contracting shape for consumer-platform integration: light-touch commercial, no traditional procurement, federated commercial relationship

CONTRACT for HealthSync is structurally different from HomeTest and HealthStore. There is no admitted supplier in the classic sense. Apple and Google operate consumer platforms the NHS reads from rather than procures from. Consumer devices are owned by the patient, not procured by the NHS. The traditional supplier-admission and call-off-contract pattern does not apply. CONTRACT for HealthSync is mostly about three things: the federated commercial arrangement with the consumer platforms, the cost model for data routing and storage at scale, and the eventual commercial frame for any AI / intelligent-navigation layer the NHS develops or procures.

The contracting shape

Federated commercial relationship with consumer platforms

In flight

Apple and Google are not NHS suppliers in the traditional sense. The commercial relationship is federated: the NHS uses platform APIs under the platform's standard developer terms; the platform passes patient data only on patient consent; the NHS pays for any usage tier that crosses commercial thresholds. The commercial pattern is closer to a strategic partnership than a procurement.

Consumer device ownership architecture

In flight

Consumer wearable and monitoring devices (BP cuffs, smartwatches, CGM sensors) are owned by the patient, procured through retail or prescription, not by the NHS as a HealthSync component. The cost falls outside the operating model. Patient device ownership is the structural foundation of HealthSync — without it, HealthSync would collapse back into HomeTest economics.

Cost model for data routing and storage

In design

At scale, the cost of routing data from consumer platforms into NHS systems and storing the longitudinal data set is non-trivial. The cost model needs to anticipate the data volume curve and the infrastructure cost shape. Sits inside the platform-build cost rather than per-configuration commercial.

AI / intelligent-navigation commercial frame

In design

If the AI / intelligent-navigation layer is built by NHS, the commercial frame is internal platform build. If it is procured from a commercial AI partner, the procurement is a separate framework arrangement. The decision sits outside current CONTRACT scope.

Contract Management for federated arrangements

In design

Contract Management discipline applies in a different shape for federated arrangements. There is no call-off to manage in the traditional sense; what is managed is the data sharing arrangement, the use-case-specific permissions, and any usage-tier commercial elements. The Contract Management capability (Cap-15 in Teamwork) covers the federated pattern.

Open decisions and dependencies
AI / intelligent-navigation build vs buy
Whether the intelligent navigation layer is built by NHS as a Foundation capability or procured from a commercial AI partner is an open structural decision. Sets the commercial frame for the largest cost variable in the HealthSync architecture.
Consumer device equity intervention
Some cohorts cannot afford the consumer devices that make HealthSync work for them. Whether the NHS provides devices to specific cohorts (and how, and under what budget) is a parallel commercial question. Sits at policy level, not in this operating model directly, but the dependency lands on contracting design.
Data routing cost trajectory
Cost model needs validation against projected adoption curves. If adoption scales faster than the cost model assumes, infrastructure investment timing becomes load-bearing.
Patient permission revocation commercial implications
When a patient revokes data sharing, the platform stops sending data immediately. The commercial implications (e.g. if there was a usage-tier crossing) need to be cleanly handled. Architecturally simple, operationally testable at Alpha.
