Aisha.

Clinical safety · universal across the portfolio
“If Abdul's result needs action, I'm the one who makes sure that action happens: safely.”
Clinical safety officer and clinical governance lead. Holds DCB0129 and DCB0160 governance for every Digital Therapeutics product. Per-product hazards differ; the framework does not. Cares about patient safety, hazard logs, clinical safety case reports, and what happens after the clinical signal lands (a test result for HomeTest, an engagement or outcome event for HealthStore).

What drives her

A near-miss that shifted her career. Aisha trained as a paediatric registrar. She was on the team during a serious-incident on her ward where a medical device behaved exactly as designed but the deployment had no operational safety wrap around it — an information failure that nearly killed a child. The child survived. The investigation took six months and reshaped how the trust deployed any new medical technology. Aisha moved out of front-line clinical work into clinical safety governance two years later. She could see the system-level fix was where the impact was.

A pattern she keeps seeing. She's watched DCB0129 and DCB0160 done well, and done badly. The badly cases all share a shape — manufacturers and deployers each thinking the other has the safety work covered, so neither owns it explicitly. Her instinct is to over-document the split, even when colleagues find it pedantic. She knows what happens when the split isn't documented.

What it adds up to. Patient safety is the only thing that matters to her professionally. Everything else is means. She doesn't see clinical safety as paperwork; she sees it as the difference between “this works” and “someone gets hurt”. She'll hold up a go-live as long as it takes for the hazard log to be honest.

Why this role matters

Without clinical sign-off, the service can't deploy. Aisha represents the clinical safety machinery: the hazard log, the DCB0129 review, the safety case. She works alongside Ngozi (IG / DPO). Clinical safety and information governance are the two parallel frameworks the platform has to satisfy at every deployment. Aisha makes the clinical activity safe. Ngozi makes the data activity lawful. The model needs both to hold for any configuration to go live.

What sits in Aisha's territory

Aisha's territory is wider than the clinical safety case alone. It covers the clinical infrastructure HomeTest depends on, the pathways HomeTest plugs into, and the institutions that own those pathways. Five things sit explicitly under her care.

Clinical safety case and hazard log
DCB0129 and DCB0160 hazard logs, supplier safety assessments, the assurance bar for any new pattern. Aisha and the Clinical Safety Group sign off whether a configuration is safe to deploy. This is the original role: everything else is the territory she has to govern around it.
Pathology labs: clinical infrastructure with a supplier overlap
Pathology labs are the clinical engine room of HomeTest. Sample analysis, result generation, turnaround times. They sit under Aisha's safety governance and in Joe's supplier territory at the same time: a grey zone the contracting model has not yet resolved. Strategic partnership with the lab network is going to be central.
Open Q for SLT: pathology lab dual position
Do pathology contracts run through the HomeTest framework as suppliers, or sit outside it as clinical infrastructure that HomeTest plugs into? The answer affects procurement vehicle, tariff treatment and the assurance bar.
EPR, SPR, FDP: the data architecture layer
HomeTest doesn't sit in a vacuum. Results have to land in patient records, clinical decisions have to be visible in care plans, and the platform has to integrate with the strategic data infrastructure that NHSE is building. Three things sit in this layer:
  • EPR landscape. System One, EMIS, Cerner, Epic. Read-write capabilities differ. HomeTest aligns to the strengths and acknowledges the weaknesses: it does not require uniform EPR behaviour to work.
  • SPR (Single Patient Record / Shared Patient Record). Where it exists, HomeTest writes to it; where it doesn't, the model falls back to point-to-point integration. Coverage varies by region.
  • PDM (Personal Data Model) as the future-state record of truth. The longer-term target for test results is the PDM, with the NHS App rendering against it natively. This supersedes the interim pattern of web hand-off into a HomeTest-hosted results UI, and over time replaces the IM1 aggregator pattern for HomeTest results. Aisha's remit is to keep the canonical models aligned so the PDM target is reachable without rework.
  • FDP (Federated Data Platform) and the events architecture. The relationship between HomeTest and FDP is currently being scoped. Canonical data models, event orchestration, and how a HomeTest test result flows through the FDP pipeline rather than around it. ChelWest insights are feeding the design. Open work: architecture strategy and commercial strategy need joining into one coherent platform story.
The ecosystem fits HomeTest in, not the other way round.
Pathway owners: GIRFT, National Cancer Programme, NHS Online
Clinical pathways aren't owned by HomeTest. They're engineered by national bodies. GIRFT (Getting It Right First Time) owns surgical and elective pathways. The National Cancer Programme owns cancer screening and diagnostic pathways. NHS Online owns digital-first journeys. HomeTest plugs into the pathways they own: not the other way round. Coordinating with pathway owners is part of Aisha's brief.
Information governance: the parallel framework
Every deployment crosses two frameworks. Aisha's clinical safety case. Ngozi's IG framework: DPIA, DSA, controller designation, lawful basis, IAO sign-off. The two frameworks overlap at specific points (clinical incidents that involve patient data, data flows that have clinical safety implications, hazard log entries with IG dimensions), and Aisha and Ngozi run them in parallel. If either framework fails, deployment can't go ahead. See Ngozi's page for the IG side of the picture.
Closed: the IAO appointment, which was a clinical-safety blocker too
The Information Asset Owner appointment was usually framed as an IG question and sat primarily with Ngozi, but it was also a clinical-safety blocker: without an IAO, the clinical safety case could not close on data-flow risks (DCB0129 hazard log entries with IG dimensions, joint clinical-IG sign-off at go-live). Closed June 2026: the role is filled (permanent NHSE employee, per PTT requirement; confirmed at the Delivery Board) and the risk register is being downgraded. The joint clinical-IG sign-off path is no longer blocked on this item.
Open Q for Martin and Richard: pharmacy-led prescribing
Pharmacy-led prescribing has its own governance (Community Pharmacy Contractual Framework), its own prescribing-safety regime, and its own IG flows between GP and pharmacy systems. It also carries different tariff implications. Sitting it under Aisha's clinical governance is a holding position. Where exactly does it fit: under Aisha, in Joe's supplier framework, or as a parallel pattern with its own owner?
