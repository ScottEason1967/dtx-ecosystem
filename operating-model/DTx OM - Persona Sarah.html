Why Sarah is here Sarah is the LA HIV commissioner. She was originally framed as the universal commissioner across multiple configurations — LA for HIV, Acute Trust for PSA-AS, ICB / GP for iron deficiency anaemia. That framing made the model look like one role, one person. In practice that is three institutions, three jobs, not one person. The model has since been refactored: Sarah holds HIV LA only. Catherine, Anita and Daniel hold the other configurations as separate commissioner personas. Ruth holds the PSA-AS monitoring-gap cohort as a TBC slot. Sarah remains as the canonical commissioner archetype — the model from which the other commissioner shapes are read.

What drives her

She came in through public health, not procurement. Sarah's first NHS role was in public health intelligence at a Public Health England team in the early 2010s. The move into commissioning came from frustration with the supply side — the public-health data told her exactly what was wrong with her population; the commissioning side never seemed to land the response at the pace or quality the data demanded. She moved to fix the supply side. She's now seen the supply side from inside for a decade.

The community she commissions for is her community. She grew up in a town not too different from the population she now commissions for. Her mother was a community nurse for thirty years. Her grandparents used the same NHS services Sarah now buys against. The moral commitment to the population isn't abstract — it's the people she knows. When she pushes back on a framework that doesn't work for low-digital-literacy patients or for unsafe-address households, she's not making a policy point. She's naming people she can think of.

Twenty-two months from board paper to first patient. The procurement scar is in the Sarah Journey but it belongs here too. Her last cardiovascular detection programme took twenty-two months from board paper to first patient. Half her career has been spent watching good clinical ideas suffocate inside good procurement processes. The Digital Therapeutics framework is the first thing she's seen that might break the pattern. If it does, the next twenty-two months produces outcomes instead of paperwork. That's what's driving her.

Why this role matters

Sarah is the LA HIV commissioner. Without her buy decision, HomeTest HIV does not scale. She holds the LA test-and-treat budget. She has the political pressure (HIV inequality targets, sexual health performance returns, value for money). She has the headcount problem: one team, multiple sexual health services to commission, no time for bespoke per-supplier contracting. The framework either makes her HIV buying decision easier or it does not. If it does not, she will buy bilaterally from her existing supplier base, the model fragments at the LA layer, and HomeTest HIV does not get past the second LA.

The model scales when each commissioner's buy decision gets simple. Pre-assured suppliers, published call-off terms, ready-made templates, single accounting route. Every step that adds bureaucracy to her side trades scale for control she didn't ask for. The framework's success metric is how many Sarahs use it without phoning their procurement lead first.

What Sarah needs from the framework

Three things, in order, all anchored to the HIV configuration. First, suppliers she does not have to clinically assure herself: the conformance certificate has to carry across to her LA without her reproducing the assurance work locally. Second, a buy mechanism that fits her existing sexual-health commissioning calendar: call-off, not full procurement, and inside her existing budget line. Third, an exit route if the HomeTest HIV configuration stops working for her cohort: she will not lock in for five years on a model she has not seen run at scale.

Why local systems will actually contract

The model assumes local systems will draw down. The real question is what they get from doing so. Seven drivers sit in Sarah's territory: not aspirational, the things her finance and operations teams already track and account for.

  • Outpatient capacity released. Every test that doesn't need a clinic visit frees the slot for someone who does. Direct, measurable, and visible in elective recovery dashboards.
  • Reduced DNAs. A kit at home doesn't get missed because of work, transport, childcare or weather. The DNA cost (clinic time, reschedule cost, downstream risk) drops.
  • Workforce shortages eased. Especially phlebotomy. Capillary draw and self-sampling routes shift the bottleneck off scarce roles.
  • Elective recovery contribution. Faster pre-op diagnostic loops, faster surveillance loops, fewer in-clinic touchpoints. Counts toward national elective recovery commitments.
  • Diagnostics backlog reduction. Routine monitoring tests don't compete with diagnostic-pathway tests for lab capacity if the model routes them appropriately.
  • Patient demand. Patients increasingly expect digital-first. Local systems that can offer it look modern; those that can't look behind.
  • Tariff reform readiness. Tariff is moving toward outcome-based and capacity-released models. Systems that can show home-test contribution to capacity are positioned for the next pricing cycle.

Value proposition is different per audience. An LA Public Health commissioner cares about inequality reach. An acute trust commissioner cares about elective capacity. An ICB commissioner cares about pathway integration. The framework has to articulate the value sharply per customer, not as a single message. Currently no single owner pushing the per-customer narrative.

The commissioner pitch in 60 seconds

The pitch Sarah hears in the first ten minutes, before she decides whether the conversation is worth continuing. Direct answers to the questions she will ask. No aspiration, no architecture, no abstractions.

Where do I save money? Clinic slots released (capacity goes to elective recovery), reduced DNAs (kit at home doesn't miss appointments), phlebotomy workload eased (capillary draw replaces venepuncture for routine monitoring), and downstream diagnostic-pathway protection (routine tests stop competing with diagnostic tests for lab capacity). The cashable saving depends on the local pathway; the released capacity counts toward national elective recovery commitments regardless.
Who's going to pay me? Not the centre. Local systems retain commissioning budgets and contract locally. The platform takes a small percent fee per transaction per transaction to sustain itself; it does not hold the commissioner contract or the patient budget. Best-practice tariffs at pathway level are how the cost / benefit dislocation between pathology, phlebotomy and primary care gets closed. This is unresolved at SLT level and on the open governance questions list.
How quickly can I get this in front of patients? Suppliers are already accredited centrally. Templates are ready (DSA, DPIA, privacy notice, clinical safety starter). The commissioner self-assesses fit, signs the call-off, and goes live. Bespoke procurement is not on the path. The lead time is days for the buy mechanism; clinical pathway changes take longer and depend on local Trust readiness.
What if it doesn't work for my cohort? Exit route is part of the call-off terms. No five-year platform lock-in. Patterns are designed to be picked up and put down as the local need changes. Switching supplier within a pattern is also supported via the supplier-switching mechanism, not by tearing up the deployment.
What if I'm not an LA? The same shape works for an acute trust (Mark's PSA-AS pattern), a primary care setting (Layla's iron deficiency anaemia pattern) or an ICB. The commissioner role is the same; the budget line and the patient cohort differ. Each commissioner type sees the value differently: LA cares about inequality reach, acute trust cares about elective capacity, ICB cares about pathway integration. The pitch needs to be sharpened per customer; that work has not yet been done.
