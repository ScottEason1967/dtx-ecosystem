A clinician's story through the operating model

Sceptic to advocate, one prescription at a time.

Ravi is a consultant respiratory physician. Twenty years in, busy COPD clinic, a list of patients longer than the time he has to see them. He's seen digital health products come and go, mostly underpowered, mostly evidence-light, mostly forgotten inside a year. Then a conditional admission lands in front of him with the kind of evidence he can defend in an MDT. This is the story of a clinician who didn't believe, prescribed anyway, and ended up in the chair where they decide what gets recommended next.

The clinician's real problem
A digital therapeutic is a clinical decision before it's anything else.
Ravi can't prescribe what he can't defend in an MDT. He doesn't mind digital tools. He minds digital tools that arrive on his desk without evidence, without integration, and without anyone able to tell him who's clinically accountable when something goes wrong. What he needs is the assurance work done before the conversation starts.
Why this operating model matters to him
Clinical confidence built once, consumed everywhere a clinician picks the product up.
The NICE EVA conditional admission, the DCB0129 manufacturer-side safety case, the DCB0160 deployer-side safety case, the IG framework and the MI feed are all done before Ravi sees the product. The bit he does is the clinical decision he was always meant to make. The operating model takes the assurance work off his desk so he can do medicine.

The patient without an option · the gap that drives the rest

Ravi has a fifty-eight-year-old patient with moderate COPD, two exacerbations in twelve months and a job that won't accommodate a daytime pulmonary rehab programme. The options on his desk don't fit her life. The conversation in clinic is the one he's had a hundred times before.

She isn't a non-compliant patient. She's a patient whose life can't accommodate the only thing I can offer her. That's a service-design problem dressed up as a compliance problem.

Ravi
What sits in front of him in clinic
Inhaler optimisation, smoking-cessation support, pulmonary rehab. Pulmonary rehab works. The evidence base is strong, the outcomes are real. But the offer is in-person, daytime, group-based and the next cohort has a six-month wait. For this patient the answer would be a structured, supported, digital self-management programme she can do around shifts. Ravi knows that programme exists in product form. He doesn't prescribe it. He doesn't have the assurance cover he'd need to defend the decision if something went wrong.
Why he's never prescribed a digital therapeutic before
No clear admission route. No defensible evidence pack. No clinical-safety case he can name. No IG pathway. No-one accountable on the supplier side that he can call. Faced with that vacuum, the cautious clinical decision is to wait. So he waits, and the patient waits, and the exacerbations keep happening.
👥
Who's in the room with him
His COPD clinical nurse specialist, his service manager, his patient. The conversation is happening at the limit of what the in-person service can offer. The next patient is waiting outside.

The conditional admission letter · the assurance cover lands on his desk

A NICE EVA conditional recommendation lands. Real-world evidence commitments attached. Clinical-safety case complete. The DTx is on the National App Formulary. For the first time Ravi has the assurance cover he's been waiting for.

It's on the formulary. NICE have looked at it. The clinical-safety case is signed. If something goes wrong I can name who's accountable. That's the cover I never had before.

Ravi
What the model is doing underneath
The NICE EVA conditionally-recommended route admits the DTx to the National App Formulary with explicit clinical evidence and a real-world evidence generation commitment attached. DCB0129 sits on the manufacturer side; DCB0160 sits on his trust's deployer side. The IG framework, supplier admission, contracting and MI are done at Foundation level. What Ravi sees in clinic is a product with a defensible evidence base, named accountability, structured outcomes capture and a clean integration into his patient's existing NHS App account. The assurance is portable: once done at the centre, consumed locally without re-litigation.
👥
What changes for him on the day it lands
The local clinical-safety officer signs the DCB0160 deployer-side case for the trust. The COPD MDT reviews the conditional admission and adds the DTx to the pathway-fit guidance. Prescribing becomes a clinical decision, not an unsupported personal risk.

The first prescription · the patient he had in mind two months ago

Same patient. Same clinic room. Different ending. Ravi prescribes the DTx from the formulary, the prescription routes through the framework, the patient activates it on her phone before she gets to her car.

Two months ago this was the conversation I couldn't finish. Today it's the conversation I can. The product was the same. What changed was the cover I had to recommend it.

Ravi
What the model is doing underneath
Prescribing happens through the clinical pathway with the formulary entry surfaced as a recommended option. The DTx is reached through the NHS App as the front door with NHS Login carrying the patient's identity. The patient consents to the data flow, the prescription event lands in the framework's MI feed and a clinical-pathway responsibility chain is in place from the moment she activates. No additional onboarding for the patient, no additional admin for Ravi, no additional risk for the trust.
👥
What the patient sees at her end
An NHS App notification. NHS Login already in place. A guided onboarding into the DTx product. The clinical pathway lead named, the support route obvious. The product feels like the NHS, not like a third-party app bolted on the side.

Watching engagement land · the bit that turns a sceptic into a believer

Twelve weeks on. The patient is engaged. Spirometry is holding. Exacerbations have dropped. Ravi opens his cohort view and sees the same pattern repeating across the fifteen patients he's prescribed for since.

It's not that the evidence changed my mind. The evidence got me to prescribe. Watching my own patients respond is what made me a believer.

Ravi
What the model is doing underneath
The MI feed from the DTx supplier lands in the framework's data layer, feeding both supplier-performance review and a clinical-view dashboard Ravi can see. Outcomes are structured: engagement, spirometry trend, exacerbation frequency, escalation events. The Ongoing Evidence Generation commitment at Foundation level means this cohort isn't just for Ravi's reassurance. It feeds NICE re-evaluation, the framework's renewal case and the next clinician's confidence at the same time. One evidence stream, three audiences.
What this didn't look like before
In the pilot era, a clinician prescribing a DTx had to take the supplier's word on outcomes. Now the outcomes feed lands in the same data spine the framework uses for everything else, structured the same way, available to him without him having to build a dashboard. The visibility is normalised.
👥
What the cohort is showing him
High engagement across most of the cohort. Two patients who disengaged in week three. The escalation triggered automatically, the CNS reached out. One patient escalated for exacerbation review at week eight. The digital pathway flagged it before it became an admission. The model is doing the job it was built to do.

Recommending to peers · the conversation that scales the adoption curve

Ravi takes the cohort to the regional respiratory MDT. Three consultants in the room who'd ignored the conditional admission notification when it landed. Two of them are prescribing within a month.

I'm not making the supplier's argument for them. I'm telling my colleagues what I've seen in my own clinic. That's a different conversation, and it lands differently.

Ravi
What the model is doing underneath
The structural choices that brought Ravi into prescribing (the NICE conditional admission, the named clinical-safety case, the assurance portability, the integrated MI) are doing the same work for every consultant in the room. None of them is being asked to recreate Ravi's decision. They're being asked to follow his trail through a structure that's already been built. Adoption scales because the bar to join doesn't move: the assurance work was done once at the centre, and Ravi's real-world experience is the social evidence that converts the next clinician.
👥
Who's in the room with him
Three regional respiratory consultants. The COPD pathway lead from a neighbouring trust. The CNS lead. The data the cohort generated, structured the same way the next consultant's cohort will be structured. This is the moment the adoption curve stops being theoretical.

What Ravi's story shows

Clinical adoption isn't a marketing problem.
It's an assurance problem solved at the centre.

The clinicians who don't prescribe digital therapeutics aren't Luddites. They're people doing a serious job who won't recommend a product they can't defend in an MDT. The operating model exists so the assurance work that makes a DTx defensible (NICE evaluation, clinical-safety cases, IG, integration, outcomes capture, named accountability) is done once at the centre and made portable. Once that's done, clinicians like Ravi prescribe, watch the outcomes land, tell their peers and end up in the chair where they shape the next admission. Adoption follows assurance.

For Ravi
A defensible clinical decision, finally.
A NICE-recommended product on the formulary. A signed safety case. A named accountability chain. Structured outcomes capture. The assurance work he could never have done alone is done once at the centre and consumed locally. The cautious clinical decision flips from wait to prescribe, and the patients he'd been running out of options for get one.
For his patients
An option that fits their life.
A structured self-management programme they can do around shifts, caring responsibilities and the rest of an actual adult life. Engagement is high, exacerbation rates are down, and the in-person services that work for some patients aren't the only thing on offer for the patients they don't fit. The service started meeting the patient where the patient already was.
For the system
Clinicians in the room where next gets decided.
The clinical reference group closes the loop: prescriber experience feeds back into which configurations are admitted, which evidence is required, which products get extended and which get withdrawn. Adoption isn't a marketing event; it's a structural one. The clinicians the model converts become the people deciding what the model admits next. The flywheel runs with clinical hands on it.
