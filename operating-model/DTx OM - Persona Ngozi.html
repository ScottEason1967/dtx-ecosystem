The cast · meet Ngozi
Universal across the portfolio. Ngozi is one of five operating roles that thread through every Digital Therapeutics product (HomeTest, HealthStore, HealthSync). The role is the same on every product; the per-product variations appear in the operational and contractual detail.

Ngozi.

Information Governance · DPO · universal across the portfolio
“Tell me who the controller is, tell me the lawful basis. Same question for every product, every configuration. Until both are clear, nothing goes live.”
NHS England Data Protection Officer and IG lead for the Digital Therapeutics platform. Holds the framework that lets patient data flow safely between the patient, the platform, the supplier and the clinical record across every product. Per-product controllers and lawful basis positions vary inside the same shared framework. Has the authority to hold up a go-live for any configuration if the data flow isn't right.

What drives her

She came at this from human rights, not compliance. Ngozi started her career as a junior lawyer working on cases involving forced data sharing in public services. The shift to NHS data protection came from wanting to be on the patient's side when data flows happen, not on the institution's side. The conviction that privacy is a public-service mission rather than a compliance overhead is older than her IG career — it's the reason she has the IG career.

A breach response that taught her. Early in her IG career at a previous trust she was on the team responding to a subject access request that went wrong. A patient's data leaked into a process that put their safeguarding status at risk. The patient was fine in the end. The trust's process got rebuilt around the lesson. She kept the lesson too — that the cost of getting IG wrong lands on the patient, not on the institution, and the patient rarely gets to know it happened.

What it adds up to. Her younger brother has a long-term condition with a record fragmented across five providers. She has watched him spend a decade explaining his history to each new clinician because the data didn't move with him. She'll hold up a HealthSync configuration's go-live for as long as it takes the data flow to be lawful, traceable and patient-controlled — because somewhere there's a Ngozi's-brother whose record is about to land in the system and the lawful basis statement is the thing that decides whether he gets to trust it.

Why this role matters

Without IG, the data can't flow. Ngozi represents the framework that makes patient data legal, safe, and auditable as it moves between the NHS App, the supplier, the lab and the clinical record. The IG layer is the second framework, alongside clinical safety. Aisha makes sure the clinical activity is safe. Ngozi makes sure the data activity is lawful. The model needs both to hold for any configuration to deploy.

What sits in Ngozi's territory

Ngozi's territory is the data side of every configuration. It covers the IG artefacts that have to be produced before deployment, the lawful basis position that lets the platform process patient data, the controller designation that decides who carries the legal risk, and the operational IG processes that have to run continuously once a configuration is live. Six things sit explicitly under her care.

DPIA, DSA, DPA: the IG artefact stack
A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) for every new configuration. Data Sharing Agreements (DSAs) between each pair of parties (patient consent, commissioner-to-supplier, supplier-to-platform, platform-to-record). Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) where a processor is appointed. These are the artefacts that have to land before any deployment goes live. Assure once, reuse nationally applies here too: standard DSA templates published, supplier completes once, deploys across commissioners without restarting the IG paperwork each time.
Controller designation and lawful basis
For every data flow on the platform, somebody is the data controller. The choice is consequential: the controller carries the legal risk, signs the lawful basis, and is accountable for the DPIA. The federated multi-controller model has NHSE as controller for the foundation (NHS App, NHS Notify identity), the commissioner as controller for the testing purpose, and the supplier as processor. The exact controller split per configuration is still being scoped, and joint controller arrangements have been ruled out.
IAO appointment and the Information Asset Register
Every information asset on the platform needs an Information Asset Owner (IAO) appointed under NHSE governance. The IAO is responsible for the data held in their asset, the access controls applied, and the audit trail. NHSE policy requires the IAO to be a permanent NHSE employee. The Information Asset Register (IAR) lists every asset, its IAO, its retention period, its access rules, and its lawful basis.
Open Q for SLT: IAO appointment as a critical blocker
The programme is heavily contractor-led. The IAO has to be a permanent NHSE employee. No eligible IAO has yet been identified. Without one, the platform cannot be formally signed off as ready to process live patient data. This is the single biggest IG blocker on the timeline.
Subject access requests, breach notification, Caldicott principles
The operational IG processes that have to run continuously once a configuration is live. Subject access requests routed through the right controller. Breach notification within statutory timescales. Caldicott principles applied to every use of patient-identifiable information. The seven Caldicott principles (justify, use only when necessary, minimum required, need-to-know, awareness of responsibilities, comply with the law, sharing is as important as protecting) sit underneath every data-flow design.
Legal directions: when one is needed and what's in the document set
A legal direction is the route used when a configuration sits outside the lawful basis the platform already carries. The trigger is configuration change: a new pathway, a new condition, a new use of data, a new controller, a new data-sharing destination. The document set is four artefacts: an approved DPIA, a technical specification, a privacy notice, and a Secretary of State letter. The DPIA is the core. The technical specification and privacy notice face the public and explain what is being done with the data. The Secretary of State letter is the formal sign-off, drafted in PTT, signed at the top of NHSE, and published online with the rest of the set. Every new configuration runs the test of whether an existing direction already covers it. If not, it goes through this gate before any data flows.
The test that decides whether a legal direction is required
Five factors decide it: what data is being used, for what purpose, who is the controller, whether the data is being matched against other datasets (secondary uses), and whether the data is anonymous or pseudonymised. Each new configuration runs the test. If a factor moves beyond what an existing direction covers, a new or amended direction is needed. If everything sits within an existing direction, the configuration deploys under it. The framework is built so the test is run early in SHAPE, not discovered at the end of CONTRACT. The widest plausible list of data controllers and data flows is asked for upfront, not bolted on later, because revising a direction mid-flight is painful.
National data infrastructure integration
HomeTest plugs into NHSE's strategic data architecture. NHS Login carries identity. NHS Notify carries communications. The Federated Data Platform (FDP) is being scoped as the events backbone. The Single Patient Record (SPR) is the canonical destination for some result types. Each of these carries its own IG framework, lawful basis, and controller designation that HomeTest has to align to rather than create afresh. How HomeTest's IG framework dovetails with FDP and SPR governance is still being scoped.
Open Q for SLT: legal directions and lawful basis
Whether HomeTest operates under direct lawful basis (commissioner consent), under legal directions issued to NHSE, or under a combination depending on configuration. The position is unresolved at SLT level and has implications for the data controller framework, the supplier contract architecture, and the assurance bar suppliers have to meet at admission.
Open Q for SLT: PTT scope is per clinical area, not per platform
PTT teams are organised by clinical area, not by the HomeTest platform. The team that decides directions for screening configurations is not the same team that decides directions for primary care, secondary care diagnostics, or screening outside the current condition. As the platform scales beyond its first clinical area, it engages multiple PTT teams in parallel, each with its own posture, process and timeline. The framework needs to be portable across PTT teams, with a clear handover pattern when a new clinical area first comes into scope. Without it, every expansion restarts the IG conversation from zero.

What Ngozi needs from the framework

Clarity on controller designation per configuration, so the lawful basis question has a defensible answer per pathway. An IAO appointed and signed in before any go-live. Standard DSA templates that can be reused across commissioners without renegotiation per deployment. A clear interface with the strategic NHSE data infrastructure (NHS Login, NHS Notify, FDP, SPR) so HomeTest is not creating parallel governance. And a clean route for breaches and subject access requests so the operational IG load doesn't fall on a project team.

It also needs an operating discipline for when IG counterparts are slow to respond. Document the working assumption, take SLT sign-off, embed it in programme governance, keep the platform moving while pressing the formal IG channel. The framework cannot stall every time a PTT or other IG body is slow to confirm a position. Working assumptions are explicit, evidenced and visible in the programme board pack, so when the formal answer arrives the gap between assumption and decision is small and traceable.

And it needs shared outcomes with delivery, not parallel ones. When IG accountability for risk and delivery accountability for outcomes pull against each other, the team gets conflicting advice, escalations stall, and informal workarounds fill the gap. The framework holds one view of what success looks like across IG and delivery, and runs a steady cadence of short, focused IG conversations through the lifecycle, rather than saving everything up for a heavyweight review at the end. Standard inputs (data flow diagram, controllers map, lawful basis statement) carry agreed quality expectations so the same evidence is not reproduced three different ways across three different reviews.
