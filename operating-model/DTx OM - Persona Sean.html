Sean.

The patient the appointment system fails · learning disability · every pathway at once
“He has had genuine real-world problems with attending medical appointments, understanding the advice, committing to the tests. HomeTest would have been a godsend.” — his brother, who works on this programme
Sean is not an archetype. He is a real man of 46 with learning disabilities and special needs, the brother of a member of the programme's commercial team, and his story is here with his family's blessing because it is the clearest answer to a question every page of this model should be able to face: who does the current system quietly fail, and what does patient-centric testing change for them? Sean stands in this model for the horizontal cohorts — the people for whom the hardest step of every pathway is the same step.

The barrier stack

For Sean, the test was never the hard part. Everything before the test was. Attending: the journey to an unfamiliar building, the waiting room with its noise and strangers and unexplained delays, the appointment that starts whether or not he is ready. Understanding: advice given quickly, in clinical language, with no time to process and nothing to take away in a form that works for him. Committing: the gap between agreeing to a test in the room and the test actually happening, which the system records as his failure. A “did not attend” code does not distinguish would not from could not, and people like Sean accumulate them — and then the system, reading non-attendance as non-engagement, quietly deprioritises exactly the people who needed the most help to attend.

What patient-centric testing changes

  • The test comes to him. Home, with someone he trusts, at his own pace. No journey, no waiting room, no stranger with a needle on a schedule that isn't his.
  • Instructions that work for him. Easy-read formats, pictures and plain words, the chance to do it again tomorrow if today went wrong. ⚑ the programme's easy-read and accessible-format approach is to be documented.
  • A supported journey, not a solo one. A carer- or family-assisted flow where ordering, doing and understanding the test can involve the person who helps him — without taking the test away from him. ⚑ design approach to be documented.
  • Adjustments that travel. The NHS reasonable-adjustment flag exists so needs like Sean's are known wherever he turns up; a patient-centric platform should read and honour it at the ordering step. ⚑ integration approach to be confirmed.
  • Results in his language. What the number means, what happens next, told plainly — to him, and with his consent to the person who supports him.

Why Sean is every pathway

Sean is not an HIV story or a PSA story or an anaemia story. He is all of them, because the barrier stack is the same in every vertical pathway this portfolio will ever run. That is what makes the horizontal cohorts — learning disability, mental health, needle-phobic patients, children and young people — a second, independent scaling axis: the vertical pathways scale conditions; the horizontal cohorts scale reach inside every condition at once. People with learning disabilities are offered an annual health check that routinely includes bloods, and it is exactly at the venepuncture step that the journey most often stalls — a cohort-shaped gap that patient-centric testing is unusually well built to close. And the curb-cut rule applies: a journey designed so Sean can complete it is a better journey for everyone — clearer instructions, calmer steps, fewer assumptions.

The approach, honestly stated. The programme has not yet documented its horizontal-cohorts approach; the senior sponsor has asked for exactly that, and much of the good practice already exists in the system and needs championing rather than inventing. This page is the why; the Horizontal Cohorts page holds the lane; the assurance standard is the enforcement hook — if inclusive kit and journey design sit inside the published standard, inclusion stops being an aspiration and becomes a condition of being on the approved list. ⚑ All of it to be confirmed by the programme.
