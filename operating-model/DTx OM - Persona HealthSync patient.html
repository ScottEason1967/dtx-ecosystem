Errol.

Patient · portfolio journey
“I didn’t feel a thing. The watch caught what I wasn’t looking at. A test landed on the doorstep, the medication was adjusted, the app helped me stick to it. I onboarded once and consented once. I never thought of any of it as three different things.”
56, Black British, lives in Manchester with his wife. Works as a tech instructor at a community college so he’s digital-comfortable without being a digital native. Diagnosed with hypertension three years ago after a near-miss at a routine medical; family history on his father’s side. Started with a connected home blood-pressure cuff and a smartwatch for the wearable layer. Eighteen months ago a watch-detected pattern led to an at-home blood test and a prescribed digital therapeutic for medication adherence. He now sits across all three products in the portfolio — HealthSync continuous, HomeTest periodically, HealthStore in life. He onboarded once. He consented once. His record stays one record. Errol is the patient whose story shows the operating model working as one model rather than three.
Why Errol is here Errol is the cross-product patient archetype — he sits across HomeTest, HealthStore and HealthSync at steady state. He exists to test the central claim of the operating model: one model, one identity, one consent, one onboarding — not three products bolted together. Other patient archetypes belong inside a single workstream. Errol crosses three. Without him the model can claim to be one platform without ever proving it. With him the test is concrete: do his handoffs work without re-onboarding, does his record stay one record, does his care team see one view? If Errol's story holds, the model is one model.

Why Errol matters in the model

Errol is the patient whose journey earns the “one operating model” claim. Other patient archetypes sit inside one workstream — Abdul, Mark and Layla on HomeTest; Pauline on HealthStore; Meera on HealthSync. Errol crosses all three. His story is the demonstration that the portfolio is not three products bolted together but one platform where a single patient’s identity, consent, clinical context and data carry across every workstream boundary without re-onboarding. He is the protagonist of the journey that proves the model.

What Errol tells the model. Three separate operating models would have forced him to onboard three times, give consent three times, exist as three patients to the NHS. The shared Foundation is what stops that happening. His story makes visible what the model has to do at every handoff: NHS App as one front door, NHS Login as one identity, one patient record across workstreams, one clinical safety framework, one IG envelope, one care-team view. The journey page is the canonical telling of his story; the rest of the model is where the components that carry him sit.

What is distinctive about Errol’s journey

Most patients touch one workstream. Errol touches three. The HealthSync layer is where it began — a connected BP cuff and a smartwatch for the wearable signal. A pattern in the watch data prompted his GP’s investigation, which triggered HomeTest (an at-home blood panel for kidney function and lipids), which led to a HealthStore prescription (a digital therapeutic for medication adherence alongside an adjusted medication regimen). All three now run concurrent for him at steady state. Each handoff happened without re-onboarding because the operating model carries his identity, consent, clinical context and clinical safety governance across the boundaries. His care team sees one view of one patient, not three signals across three systems they have to reconcile. The story has its own dedicated page because it is the centrepiece argument of the model, not just another patient instance.
