Ravi.

Clinical · HealthStore prescriber
“I prescribe the app the same way I’d prescribe pulmonary rehab. The difference is the patient gets it on their phone the next day.”
48, consultant respiratory physician at a mid-sized district general. Trained at a London teaching hospital, now runs the COPD service. Sees patients like Pauline every clinic. Was sceptical of digital therapeutics for years but the conditional admission route gave him enough cover to try it. His clinic is an early-adopter site for HealthStore COPD; he’s now in the regional steering group helping the next two trusts onboard. Operates within the OPTIMISE framework for COPD: eight processes of care designed to prevent deterioration, exacerbation and hospital admission. Takes a biopsychosocial view — addresses psychological, physical and social drivers of condition, not just the biomedical signs — which is exactly the shape a digital therapeutic wrapped in a proper service layer can support.

Why Ravi matters in the model

Ravi is the activator of every HealthStore episode. The digital therapeutic does not reach the patient without a prescribing decision; the prescribing decision sits in a clinical workflow that has its own incentives, time pressures and accountability. The COPD service uses risk-stratification searches to identify rising-risk patients (for instance: a COPD diagnosis plus two or more courses of oral steroids in the last twelve months); the digital therapeutic is one of the interventions the prescribing clinician can deploy from that point. If prescribing is not embedded in the natural clinical journey, HealthStore stays a curiosity. If it is, HealthStore scales. Ravi is the unit of demand on the supply side of the model.

What Ravi tells the model. The prescribing flow has to be easier than the alternative. Three barriers shape the model: workflow fit (the prescription has to slot into the patient consultation without adding clinical time), evidence fit (the clinician has to be confident the digital therapeutic is clinically sound under the conditional admission route), and outcome fit (the clinician has to see results at follow-up that make repeat prescribing rational). Each barrier shapes a different layer of the architecture: workflow fit shapes the platform integration, evidence fit shapes the assurance gateway, outcome fit shapes the measurement loop.

What is distinctive about Ravi’s role

Ravi is a clinician acting in a prescribing capacity, not a clinical safety officer. The HealthStore clinical safety governance sits with the Clinical Safety Group and the DCB framework (DCB0129 manufacturer side with the admitted supplier; DCB0160 deployer side with the deploying organisation). Ravi operates inside that framework but his role is operational: choose the right patients, hand over the therapeutic with appropriate clinical context, monitor engagement, interpret outcome, decide what to do next. He sits at the third step of the three-step selection model (DTx Approver → Commissioner → Clinician): the digital therapeutic has already been admitted nationally and commissioned locally; Ravi makes the call at the point of care. The clinician-initiated escalation route (CIE) lets him pull a patient out of the supported-self-management tier back to clinical contact where the therapeutic surfaces concern. The model has to give him the visibility, the evidence and the workflow integration he needs to prescribe with confidence.
