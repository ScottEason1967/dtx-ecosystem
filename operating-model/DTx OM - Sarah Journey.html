Easy buying. Find. Decide. Onboard. Go live. Operate.
Commissioners don't buy digital therapeutics for the joy of running procurements. They buy them to close a detection gap, lower an admission rate, or get more out of a stretched workforce. The operating model exists so the buying gets out of the way and the population outcome stays in focus. Easy buying is the North Star, not because procurement should be lazy, but because effort spent on procurement is effort not spent on population health.

The brief · a population, a gap, a year to close it

Sarah has a cardiovascular detection gap and a director asking what digital can do about it. She has budget and a timeline. She does not have eighteen months for three procurements.

I've got a gap I can see on the dashboard, money on the table and twelve months to show movement. What I don't have is the headcount to run three separate procurements end-to-end.

Sarah
What she walks in with
A population brief covering cardiovascular detection, COPD admission and a high-risk cohort needing closer monitoring. The clinical question is settled: HomeTest for CVKM screening, a HealthStore COPD therapeutic, HealthSync for the wearable layer on the high-risk cohort. What's in front of her is procurement, integration and assurance. This is the moment the operating model has to earn its keep.
The old way she's done this before
Three separate procurements, each taking six to nine months. Three sets of clinical-safety assurance to verify. Three IG assessments. Three integration projects. Three sets of MI. A year and a half if everything ran in parallel; closer to two if it didn't. The last cardiovascular detection programme she stood up before the framework existed took twenty-two months from board paper to first patient.
👥
What her team is looking at
Detection-gap analysis, cohort sizing, budget envelope, timeline. Clinical case settled with the relevant pathway teams. Procurement and integration ahead, owners not yet assigned.

One front door · the framework opens

She opens the framework. Three product overlays. Configurations admitted. Suppliers on the catalogue. National terms. Prices visible. No procurement to design from scratch.

I expected to be writing a spec. I'm looking at suppliers I can already call off. The framework did the heavy lifting before I logged in.

Sarah
What the model is doing underneath
A coherent set of contracting instruments carries all three product overlays — different per product, common shape. HomeTest suppliers admitted via UKAS / ISO lab accreditation and the supplier admission gate (Step 1 interim route in the current window: six-month SBS PSR bridge for HIV; Step 2 steady-state vehicle under SLT alignment). HealthStore products admitted via the NICE EVA conditionally-recommended route into the National App Formulary (ACE plus NHS Standard Contract). HealthSync configurations admitted via consumer-platform integration agreement. Eligibility Filters let Sarah filter for products that fit her pathway, her IG profile, her DCB0160 deployer readiness. The contracting instruments, the Assurance Model and the Pattern catalogue are all Foundation, built once, reused per commissioner.
👥
What her team is looking at
Catalogue view across three product overlays. Admitted suppliers per overlay with clinical-safety, regulatory and IG evidence packs ready to consume. Maximum pricing visible. Call-off templates pre-loaded.

The fit assessment · whether each configuration actually works for her ICB

The Self-Assessment Toolkit asks four questions of each configuration. Pathway fit. IG fit. Prescribing alignment. DCB0160 readiness. Sarah and her team work through them inside a fortnight.

It told me where I needed to do work, not where to start writing a spec from scratch. Three of the four questions came back clean. The fourth had a mitigation route ready to go.

Sarah
What the model is doing underneath
The Self-Assessment Toolkit is commissioner-facing: the same four-question filter used across HomeTest, HealthStore and HealthSync. Behind it sits the Assurance Model coordinating Independent Assurance, NICE EVA, DTAC, DCB0129 (manufacturer) and DCB0160 (deployer). Suppliers have already been through the upstream assurance bar; Sarah's work is verifying local fit, not re-doing assurance someone else has already passed. Assurance portability at Foundation level is what makes this cheap: evidence done once at the centre, consumed many times by commissioners.
The old way she's done this before
A 60-page supplier due-diligence template per supplier. Clinical-safety case review from scratch. DPIA written cold. Pathway-fit assessment done by a working group across three months. Today: a four-question toolkit per configuration, evidence packs pre-loaded, a fortnight of focused work with the right people in the room.
👥
Who's in the room with her
Her clinical-safety officer, her IG lead, the prescribing pathway lead for the COPD configuration, and her cardiovascular pathway lead. Plus the framework's commissioner-engagement contact for the open question.

One call-off · the contract that wasn't six contracts

Call-off under the NHS Standard Contract with SaaS and data schedules. One legal instrument. Multiple suppliers underneath it. The same payment milestone structure across all three workstreams.

One signature. Same legal instrument. Same milestone structure. The procurement we'd expected to lose six months to was done in six weeks.

Sarah
What the model is doing underneath
Call-off agreement under the NHS Standard Contract with SaaS and data schedules. The same legal instrument carries the HomeTest CVKM configuration, the HealthStore COPD therapeutic and the HealthSync wearable cohort. National terms, max pricing and supplier admission rules already baked into the framework agreement above her call-off. Payment milestone structure is shared across all three workstreams: mobilisation, activity, volume floor where it applies; per-episode against milestone evidence where it doesn't. NHS Commercial (David) sits behind the contract architecture as part of the platform. NHS Legal sits behind the framework architecture above her. She isn't negotiating either. She's calling off against them.
👥

What Sarah's story shows

Three workstreams, one operating model.
Three changes, for three audiences.

Three separate operating models would have forced Sarah to run three procurements, write three business cases, manage three integrations and reconcile three sets of MI. Her year would have gone on the plumbing, not the population. The operating model exists so that none of that happens, and so that the things that do happen are the ones that move outcomes for the people she commissions for.

For Sarah
A year not lost to procurement.
Six weeks from brief to call-off, not eighteen months. One legal instrument, not six. One MI feed, not three. The procurement, integration and assurance work she would have done locally has been done once at the centre and reused. The time she got back went into the next problem on her list, not into chasing supplier reporting templates.
For her population
Care arriving faster.
Detection earlier in the cardiovascular cohort. Admission rates lower in the COPD cohort. Passive monitoring catching high-risk patients before crisis. The patients didn't see a procurement. They saw a service in the NHS App. The eighteen months she didn't spend on the back office is the eighteen months they didn't spend waiting.
For the system
A pattern, not a one-off.
What Sarah did, the next commissioner does without rebuilding it. The framework, the assurance evidence, the contract architecture, the MI feed, built once, reused many times. Sarah's outcomes feed into the evidence flywheel that admits the next supplier, supports the next NICE re-evaluation and informs the next best-practice tariff. Easy buying scales.
One commissioner. One signature. One year. The model carried the weight.
