Anatomical · what it is composed of

Foundation, overlays, patterns, configurations

The Digital Therapeutics operating model is built once at the bottom and varied at the top. The foundation is universal across every product. The overlays are the eight dimensions where products and configurations diverge. The patterns are the options inside each overlay. The configurations are the real-world deployments. This is the architecture that lets one platform support HomeTest, HealthStore and HealthSync today, with multiple configurations inside each, without rebuilding the foundation. The catalogue further down shows the picks per product across all three columns.

01North stars: the three principles every design choice is checked against
Mission · for the patients of every Digital Therapeutics product
The operating model exists so any Digital Therapeutics service can reach patients safely, work at scale, and deliver outcomes people can act on. The three north stars below are the operational consequences of that: what has to be true of the people running, buying and selling the service, so that mission can be met for the patient. Each product carries its own patient archetypes (HomeTest: Abdul, Mark, Layla. HealthStore: COPD patient archetype. HealthSync: in design) inside the same architecture.
North star for commissioners · Sarah
Easy buying
Find. Decide. Onboard. Go live. Operate. Ready-made templates and route-to-buy. No bespoke setup per commissioner.
North star for suppliers · Joe
Easy onboarding
Self-service first. Onboard once per API. Lighter per test. Published criteria. Tiered routes so smaller suppliers stay in scope.
North star for the platform · Priya
Matchmaker, not counterparty
Sets standards, runs onboarding, manages the framework, builds integration, monitors market health. Does not sign local contracts, own billing, deliver care, or operate logistics.

Read the Foundation carefully
The Foundation level has two halves that are easy to conflate. Some foundation elements the platform builds, runs and staffs (integration spine, contracting instrument, IG framework structure, hazard log structure). The contracting instrument in the current window is the Step 1 interim route (six-month SBS PSR bridge for HIV; Alpha/Beta MOUs continuing for live deployments); the Step 2 steady-state vehicle decision (PA23 vs PSR not yet settled in law) is under SLT alignment. Others are shared NHS services the platform plugs into rather than runs (NHS Login, NHS Notify, NHS App, FHIR routing). The first half is the BAU team's job to operate. The second half is the BAU team's job to interface with, govern and configure. The full ecosystem of consumed, regulated, contracted and served entities sits on the Ecosystem page.

Service Wrapper anatomy The Service Wrapper Foundation item decomposes into a six-touchpoint patient chain the patient experiences, plus four local-pathway integration items where the wrapper meets local clinical operating reality. Holds across all Digital Therapeutics products with product-specific adaptation per touchpoint. Click any item to expand
The six-touchpoint patient chain in sequence · the patient experiences each in order
1 Referral / InviteHow a patient enters a Digital Therapeutics service.
Could be a GP referral (Layla's iron deficiency anaemia), a clinician-initiated order (Mark's PSA-AS), a patient-initiated self-presentation via the NHS App (Abdul's CVKM screening), or a system-prompted invite based on PHR data (Errol's BP threshold). The wrapper holds the route-in across all of these so the patient enters one front door regardless of trigger.
2 Login & onboardIdentity verification, consent, eligibility check.
NHS Login carries identity. Consent is captured once per patient per product. Eligibility check against the configuration's clinical criteria. A patient who has logged in once for HomeTest doesn't re-onboard for HealthStore or HealthSync — the “onboard once” claim of the operating model depends on this layer holding.
3 In-life Help & SupportWhat happens when the patient needs help mid-journey.
Kit not arrived. Result confusing. App not working. Prescription unclear. Multi-modal — phone, NHS App chat, signposting to local pathway, escalation routes. The wrapper carries this so the patient doesn't bounce between supplier helplines and NHS routes depending on which product they're using. Critical equity-of-access function.
4 Use & MotivationThe engagement layer between clinical touchpoints.
Reminders, nudges, behaviour-change support, progress signals. The mechanism varies per product (kit-return reminders for HomeTest, adherence prompts for HealthStore, sustained-use nudges for HealthSync) but the principle is constant: the wrapper holds the patient between clinical touchpoints so the service keeps delivering when the clinician isn't in the room.
5 Signposting / OffboardingOnward routing and clean exit.
When the journey reaches a natural end (test complete, course finished, condition resolved) or when the patient needs to be pointed elsewhere (onward referral, return to GP, escalation to urgent care). Defined exit pathways and onward routing into the rest of the NHS — not a dead end at the edge of the digital service.
6 Data returnClosing the loop into the patient record and the evidence pipeline.
Data flows back into the patient record, to the referring clinician, to MI / population dashboards and to the evidence pipeline that feeds the Assurance Model and Ongoing Evidence Generation. The wrapper ensures data closes the loop, not just collects it. Without this layer the model produces activity, not value.
Local-pathway integration items where the wrapper meets local clinical operating reality
Local use supportFace-to-face support for patients who can't or won't self-serve digitally.
Community health workers, pharmacy support, NHS Online phone help. Critical for equity-of-access — the wrapper assumes not every patient is digital-first. Particularly load-bearing for HomeTest where unsafe-address, digital-exclusion and motivation patterns are explicit operating constraints.
Remote / Passive monitoringThe clinician-side view of patient journeys without active intervention.
Cohort-level dashboards, threshold-based alerting, anomaly detection. Particularly load-bearing for HealthSync where the passive data flow is the point of the product. Less prominent for HomeTest but still present at the population-MI layer.
PI / CI escalation modelPatient-initiated and clinician-initiated escalation paths.
The patient says “something's wrong” (PI) or the clinician says “this needs attention now” (CI). Defined routing from either side into the clinical pathway, with clear handover protocols. A safety-critical construct — without it, signals get lost between the digital service and the clinical responders.
Neighbourhood Health team integrationHand-off between the wrapper and local Neighbourhood Health teams.
Care coordinators, neighbourhood clinicians, place-based teams. The handover from digital to in-person, and the inverse. Becoming load-bearing as Neighbourhood Health teams roll out from late 2026. The wrapper is the interface; the NH team is the operating partner.
Note on terminology. A reframing from “Service Wrapper” to “Human Wrapper” is in flight. The model carries Service Wrapper today; vocabulary alignment to follow when the reframing lands authoritatively.

Emerging pattern at L3 · Supply chain Collection-first as alternative to delivery-first. Today HomeTest assumes delivery-first (kit posted to patient, patient returns by post). A collection-first pattern (pharmacy or community-hub pickup) removes the 5-7 day delivery wait, addresses unsafe-address barriers and reduces motivation decay between order and action. Signal that this pattern is arriving from outside the framework: another NHS programme has independently inserted “collect from pharmacy” into journeys lifted from HomeTest screens. Open question is whether to formalise collection-first as a named Supply chain pattern alongside All-inclusive at L3, selectable per configuration.
What's not yet positioned Scope items that have surfaced through review but haven't been placed in the model yet. Each needs an SLT or sponsor decision before it gets a home in the foundation, overlays, or patterns.
  • Neighbourhood Health Centres. Could be a delivery channel (kit distribution / sample collection), a referral source (clinicians there initiate testing), or both. SLT to confirm.
  • Adoption: where does it sit structurally? Adoption activity is being done through the Partnerships workstream, and is two-sided: customer-side partnerships (GIRFT, NHS Online, national programmes that drive demand) and supply-side partnerships (assuring labs, supporting capillary centres, bringing the right supplier mix to market). Customer success is a third dimension: making demand and supply work together once both are in the room. Adoption itself runs through early-adopter trusts that benefit commercially from being first movers: they build the capability, consult into other trusts, and become peer advocates. Peer-to-peer credibility carries further than central messaging. What hasn't been resolved is whether adoption appears as a named function in the operating model itself (an overlay, a foundation capability, a row in the RACI), or stays implicit inside Partnerships. Decision: SLT to confirm whether to surface adoption structurally.
  • Mobilisation and “hearts and minds”. Transformation requires people to change all of these: workflows, clinical assumptions, operating patterns, commissioning behaviours, patient interaction models, risk tolerance, even professional identity. Not soft work; core strategic infrastructure. The platform succeeds only if clinicians trust it, pathology networks back it, commissioners see value, suppliers believe the market is viable, patients feel safe, national programmes advocate for it, and Trusts see measurable operational benefit. Currently flagged but not owned in the model.
  • Clinician workflow integration. Acute-initiated and GP-initiated patterns require ordering and result handling inside the clinician's existing EPR or clinical system, not a second screen. The genomics service learned this the hard way: a separate ordering UI added integration complexity and was unpopular with clinicians. Single integration into the clinical workflow is a hard design constraint for acute-init (Mark's PSA-AS journey) and GP-init (Layla's iron deficiency anaemia journey). The model accommodates it through the result-handling overlay, but the integration burden on the supplier and the platform is real and not yet sized.
  • Segmented strategic messaging. Different value proposition per audience (clinicians, commissioners, suppliers, patients, national programmes), not a single narrative for all. The model has not yet developed audience-specific articulation. Owner: TBC.

Build vs buy
The overlays are also where build vs buy gets decided. Each of the eight dimensions resolves into either a platform-build capability (the centre builds it once and every commissioner inherits it), a buy-from-market capability (suppliers compete to deliver it within the framework), or a local-system responsibility (the commissioner or Trust owns it). Initiation pattern, result handling and information governance lean toward platform-build (universal mechanics). Supply chain, commercial structure and tariff treatment lean toward buy-from-market. Sign-off route and accreditation bar sit with local systems or accrediting bodies. This is the level at which the BA + product owner work plays out: each overlay needs a build-buy-borrow decision before the platform requirements are stable. That decision has not yet been made for every overlay.
Open architectural decision · DCB0160 LA assurance scale-mode
The Sign-off overlay carries an open architectural choice on how DCB0160 deployer assurance scales when there are dozens of LA commissioners. Three options on the table (Delivery Board, 26 May 2026): (i) inform LAs and leave them to self-assess; (ii) adopt the Wayfinder question set with CSO sign-off; (iii) a centrally-led safe-deployment model where LAs reuse a blueprint. The choice is not currently codified at overlay level. Option (iii) would materially change Cap-2 (Safety) by shifting load to the centre and would land in Play 10 (Local commissioner) of the Commercial Playbook. No recommendation yet.
Sample-collection device constraint
Postal-return viability depends on the device. Transdermal sample-collection devices (Tasso+, Tap, Impress) do not fit Royal Mail Large Letter internal depth dimensions. Any configuration that selects a transdermal device cannot use Large Letter for return, which constrains the supply-chain pattern choice and pushes return logistics into a different size class or carrier route. Conventional capillary-draw and finger-prick kits fit Large Letter. The constraint is physical and lives at the overlay level, not at the configuration level: it pre-empts pattern selection wherever a transdermal device is the right clinical fit.
Note on initiation channel terminology
Patient-activated handed-out kits are a digital pathway variant, not the non-digital channel. Kits that are handed out in person (at a pharmacy, an outreach event, a community setting) and then activated by the patient online still run on the digital infrastructure. They sit inside the digital channel as a variant of patient-init. The true non-digital channel is telephony: a patient who phones in, never touches the digital surface, and has the test arranged on their behalf. That channel remains a separate gap to be designed for equity and for GDS inclusion, and should not be confused with handed-out kit variants.
Note on trend analysis
Longitudinal trend analysis is deprioritised for now. PSA values from capillary samples and from venous samples are not directly comparable, so presenting them on a single trend line carries a clinical safety risk: a patient could be reassured or alarmed by an apparent rise or fall that is actually a modality effect. The position is to research the comparability question rather than build trend rendering into the platform at this phase. Result handling continues to expose individual results clearly; longitudinal interpretation stays with the treating clinician until the research lands.
Note on result shape
Result shape is a condition characteristic, not a pattern. HIV gives a binary outcome (reactive / non-reactive). PSA gives a continuous numerical value, often interpreted in trend during active surveillance. Nobody picks a result shape; it's intrinsic to the condition. It does, however, influence which Result handling pattern is sensible (binary leans Patient-direct, continuous leans Clinician-first) and shapes the hazards loaded into the clinical safety case (missed positive vs range misinterpretation are different failure modes). The platform carries both shapes via FHIR Observation without modification; the variation lives in the patient-facing rendering and the hazard log content.
