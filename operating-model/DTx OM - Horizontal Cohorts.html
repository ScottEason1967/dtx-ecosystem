Horizontal Cohorts.

The portfolio scales two ways. The vertical way is the one the model already tells: conditions and pathways, HIV then PSA, fast followers within each stream, pattern followers across them. The horizontal way cuts across all of it: the cohorts of people for whom every pathway's hardest step is the same step — getting to the appointment, being understood, completing the test. Serve them once, and you have served them in every condition this portfolio will ever carry.

The four named cohorts

People with learning disabilities

Offered an annual health check that routinely includes bloods, and it is at the venepuncture step that the journey most often stalls. Unfamiliar settings, clinical language, journeys that depend on support being available at the system's chosen hour. Sean is this cohort's face in the cast — a real story, told with his family's blessing.

People living with serious mental illness

Carry some of the worst physical-health outcomes in the system and some of the highest barriers to clinic attendance — and are exactly the group for whom physical-health checks, including bloods, are most consistently missed. Testing that comes to the person, on a day they can manage, changes the denominator.

Needle-phobic patients

A barrier that hides inside every “did not attend” statistic and is rarely coded as what it is. Patient-centric sampling — finger-prick today, transdermal as it proves out — is not just more convenient for this cohort; it is the difference between tested and not tested.

Children and young people

Their own consent framework, their own settings, their own relationship with needles and clinics. A cohort where familiar surroundings and a trusted adult present are not comfort features but the conditions under which testing happens at all.

Two axes, one portfolio

Vertical · conditions

HIV, PSA, iron deficiency anaemia, COPD, cardiac rehab. Scale by adding pathways and fast followers within them; the pattern carries the machinery from one condition to the next.

Horizontal · cohorts

Learning disability, serious mental illness, needle phobia, children and young people. Scale by widening who completes each pathway. Every vertical inherits the gain at once — reach, not conditions.

The horizontal axis is also the strongest health-inequalities story the portfolio owns, and it is largely a championing job rather than an invention job: much of the good practice already exists in the system and needs picking up, documenting and marketed as an opportunity for other organisations to adopt. External recognition already exists — the programme has been publicly called out as an enabler in this space. ⚑ The named good-practice sources and the recognition reference are to be confirmed into this page by the programme.

Where the lane bites in the model

  • The assurance standard is the enforcement hook. If inclusive kit and journey design (easy-read instructions, carer-assisted flows, reasonable-adjustment handling) sit inside the published standard, inclusion becomes a condition of the approved list, assessed through the same mechanism as everything else — assure once, deploy many, include everyone. ⚑ proposed, for the assurance owner to shape.
  • The ordering step honours the reasonable-adjustment flag. The NHS flag exists so needs travel with the patient; a patient-centric platform should read it where the order is placed. ⚑ integration approach to be confirmed.
  • The personas carry the dimension. Sean leads it; the cohort lens applies inside every patient story in the cast, because the barrier stack lives inside every pathway.
  • The economic story compounds. Every cohort member reached at home is a missed-appointment cycle avoided and a health-inequalities outcome the commissioner can count. The curb-cut rule does the rest: journeys designed for the hardest case are better journeys for everyone.
