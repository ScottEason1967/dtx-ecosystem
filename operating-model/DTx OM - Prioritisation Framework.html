Prioritisation lens · HomeTest decision tool

How we choose what's next.

The HomeTest Prioritisation Framework is the lens we use to decide which test pathways move forward, and in what order. It sits alongside the operating model. The OM describes what the system is; this framework describes which parts we build first. Three levels: 5 Patterns, 4 Tiers, and a Prioritisation Construct for ranking specific use cases.

Endorsed by the HomeTest Programme Board, 9 June 2026

Why a separate lens. The operating model answers architectural questions: where do things sit, how do they connect, who is accountable. The Prioritisation Framework answers governance questions: what do we take forward, what do we delay, what do we deprioritise, and on what evidence. The two need to be readable separately because they get used by different people, in different rooms, against different questions.

What it gets used for. Pattern selection at the architectural level, configuration sequencing inside HomeTest's roadmap, use-case scoring at the Programme Board, and a defensible record of why one condition is in scope this year and another is held back. The framework does not make the decision. It structures the evidence so the decision can be made on the same axes every time.

Level 1 5 Patterns

What type of pathway are we dealing with?

Five named access patterns. Each one is a distinct shape of pathway, distinguished by who initiates the test, where the clinical ownership sits, what the pathway shape looks like, and the strategic role the pattern plays for the platform.

A test pathway has to fit one of these five patterns before any other prioritisation question is worth asking. The pattern determines who orders, who reads, who acts, and how the result returns to the record. The five patterns are not in priority order. They are five different shapes the platform has to be able to serve.

Level 2 4 Tiers

How suitable is this pathway for delivery now?

Four tiers classify a pathway on the clarity of clinical ownership, the complexity and variation in delivery, and the strength of the test-to-decision-to-action loop. Tier 1 pathways are closed-loop and ready; Tier 4 are too variable to take on in the near term.

Tier is not about importance. It is about delivery-readiness. A Tier 4 condition can be the right next target once the pathway clarity comes; today it would simply break the model. The four tiers are an honest filter that stops the platform spending design effort on configurations whose underlying pathway is not yet stable.

Tier 1
Clean
What it isClosed-loop, fully owned pathways. Fast-follow candidates.
Operational shapePredictable and repeatable. Clear test-to-decision-to-action chain.
Why it mattersBest candidates for proving the model fast. Lowest risk of pathway-side surprises during build.
Tier 2
Strong
What it isSame model as Tier 1 with some complexity layered on.
Operational shapeShared care, complex result panels, or clinical interpretation steps in the loop.
Why it mattersReal-world applicability. Demonstrates the model can serve the pathway shape most of the NHS actually runs.
Tier 3
Valid
What it isThe model works but the shape is different from the proving cases.
Operational shapeEpisodic, operational, or lower addressable market.
Why it mattersWorth supporting but does not prove the model cleanly. Reach matters more than rigour here.
Tier 4
Complex
What it isHigh variation, unclear ownership, multi-step pathway.
Operational shapeHard to standardise. Pathway clarity not yet there.
Why it mattersNot suitable for early delivery. Worth tracking but should not enter the roadmap until the pathway settles.
Level 3 Prioritisation Construct

Which specific use cases do we take forward?

Within a chosen pattern and tier, the Prioritisation Construct scores specific candidate use cases. One Gateway plus three scored Dimensions plus risk overlays.

Each candidate use case is first put through the Gateway. If the Gateway is failed, the use case stops there. Use cases that pass are scored on three Dimensions (Strategic, Pathway, Economic) at 1, 3 or 5. Non-scored overlays are reviewed separately and can override scoring on a case-by-case basis.

Gateway 1 · pass / fail
Clinical, technical and platform effort
Before scoring starts, the use case has to pass a viability check. Is home sampling clinically appropriate. Is the lab processing scalable. Is the clinical safety case credible. Is the acceptability of an at-home pathway plausible for the cohort. A use case that fails the Gateway is not scored; it leaves the construct here.
Decision shape: pass / fail. No partial credit. A use case that fails on any limb of the Gateway is held until the limb can be answered.
Dimension A · scored 1 / 3 / 5
Strategic and Policy Alignment
Scored on three sub-axes. A1: explicit national policy linkage. A2: programme ownership and commitment from a system actor. A3: criticality to policy delivery (is this load-bearing for a published priority).
A1 · Policy linkage
Explicit named link to NHS or DHSC policy.
A2 · Programme ownership
A national programme has named this in their commitments.
A3 · Criticality
Other priorities lean on this being delivered.
Dimension B · scored 1 / 3 / 5
Pathway Maturity and Readiness
Scored on three sub-axes. B1: pathway is defined and there is clinical consensus. B2: initiation pattern is mature (the Level 1 pattern this use case sits inside is well-understood at scale). B3: implementation capability exists (GIRFT, NHSO, Cancer Alliances or equivalent).
B1 · Pathway definition
Clinical pathway is defined with consensus.
B2 · Pattern maturity
The initiation pattern is operationally mature.
B3 · Implementation capability
A delivery vehicle exists to land this at scale.
Dimension C · scored 1 / 3 / 5
Economic and System Impact
Scored on three sub-axes. C1: Total Addressable Market for the test. C2: volume and demand signal today. C3: pathway impact share and strategic alignment to wider system efficiency.
C1 · TAM
Size of the addressable population.
C2 · Volume and demand
Current activity level and projected growth.
C3 · Pathway impact
Strategic alignment to the wider system efficiency case.
Risk overlays · not scored
Reviewed separately
Sit alongside the scored Dimensions and can override the score. Reviewed at the Programme Board on a case-by-case basis.
Portfolio concentration Political / programme sensitivity Pre-mortem risk Out-of-control dependencies Supplier risk

How this connects to the operating model

The five patterns map to the Level 3 Initiation row on the Anatomical page. The four tiers and the Prioritisation Construct score sit alongside each Level 4 configuration. The framework is the reason we are building the configurations we are building, in the order we are building them. The OM is the architecture. This is the prioritisation logic that drives which architectural slots get filled, and when.

What the framework has been used to deprioritise

The framework is most useful as a forcing function for honest no's. Use cases that score low on Dimensions A or B (no clear policy linkage, no defined pathway, no named delivery vehicle) get shown to be deprioritisation candidates on the same axes that promote others. The construct is symmetrical: the same score that elevates one use case is the score that delays another.

Examples held against the construct: use cases where the Gateway has not yet been cleared (clinical safety case incomplete, lab processing not scalable), use cases where Dimension B is too thin (pathway not yet defined at consensus level), and use cases where the risk overlays carry political or programme sensitivity that requires a separate Programme Board decision.
