HomeTest Operational Swimlanes (HomeTest overlay)

How the national HomeTest service runs at steady state. This page is the HomeTest operational overlay within the Digital Therapeutics Operating Model. A portfolio Operational hub page (with HealthStore and HealthSync overlays alongside) will sit above this. Seven operational processes, seven actors. The patient is one of them: Abdul, Mark, Layla, and everyone like them. The other six are the people who get the right test to the right person, return the right result, and trigger the right next step.

How to read this

Seven operational lanes, each describing a distinct process at steady state. Each lane has the same seven actors. Where a cell is empty, that actor genuinely has no role at that stage.

This is the steady-state national operating model, not the route to get there. Discovery / Alpha / Beta selection processes sit outside scope. The live service support model carries two tiers: a Day-1 tier covering beta cutover and early-life support, and the steady-state tier shown on this page from go-live (April onward). Day-1 mechanics are interim. Where steady-state behaviour is different, the steady-state behaviour is what the lanes describe.

Three routes

Route A: Standard. Full bar across all five dimensions. End-to-end capability against an API. Scaled tier on every dimension. Default route for suppliers offering end-to-end test delivery.

Route B: Light-touch. Constrained scope (single test, single LA pilot, PoC). Core tier across all dimensions; scaled tier on dimensions material to scope. Stops smaller suppliers being priced out by balance-sheet alone.

Route C: Partnership. NHS provider partners and acute use case partners. Design-and-validation partnership rather than commercial supplier contract. IP vests in NHSE. Distinct criteria reflecting the partnership archetype.

Growth pathway. Route B can progress to Route A by demonstrating scaled-tier evidence through live operation over a defined period. Annual default review or triggered by material change.

Why this design. Onboard once per API, lighter-weight per test. Publish the criteria; don't gate them. Self-assessment first, human review second. Tiered thresholds so the floor doesn't exclude. No defensive filtering. Every artefact exists once. Supplier experience is a design input. Anchored in the working position that a good supplier onboarding model is mostly self-service: the centre has to make it really easy for suppliers to bring themselves in, not act as a bottleneck.

Open decisions

Royal Mail Health terms. Volume discounts apply per shipper rather than aggregated across NHS suppliers. The only way to leverage those terms would be for NHS to centralise kit fulfilment and stock management. Not viable without fundamental model change.

Panel-of-tests sequencing. How panels of tests are introduced to commissioners over time, and the order in which they enter the catalogue, not yet defined.

Supplier offboarding. Onboarding is one half of the supplier lifecycle. Voluntary exit and performance-driven removal are covered in Lane 6 (Market dynamics) alongside resilience and market evolution. Detailed offboarding mechanics (notice periods, evidence thresholds, appeals, commissioner transition support) are still to be designed.

NHS Online payment flow as reference architecture. The payment plumbing for an analogous central service uses standard NHS mechanisms. ICB host ↔ NHS Online via NHS Standard Contract: the host ICB carries the central relationship. ICB local ↔ Diagnostic Provider via PbR (Payment by Results — activity-driven payment), CDS (Commissioning Dataset — the activity record), SUS (Secondary Uses Service — the national activity store) and HRG (Healthcare Resource Group — the grouped tariff unit): a local commissioner pays the provider per recorded activity through the standard hospital-data flow. NHSBSA (NHS Business Services Authority, which runs the Drug Tariff) ↔ Pharmacy via Drug Tariff: the prescription side pays the pharmacy through the existing national mechanism. OPCS-4 (the procedure coding standard) classifies the activity. The friction point: a central platform generates local activity that local commissioners did not plan or contract for. Same gap HomeTest faces at scale.
The tariff position. No live NHS tariff code yet covers home testing, but a Patient-Not-Present tariff design is now in flight. NHSE Strategic Finance has drafted a PSA Active Surveillance Monitoring Episode tariff with a glide-path: shadow 26/27, dual-run at 50% in 27/28, live at 100% in 28/29. Five reconciliation mechanisms sit underneath it (bundled episode, efficiency-factor offset, glide-path, capacity-release credits, ICB-level capitated AS top-up). The longer-term "no tariff code" framing is therefore wrong in flavour for PSA; for HIV the LA budget route remains the position. Planned volumes and opt-in commissioning still apply where the tariff has not yet landed.

Open decisions

Capacity ceilings and overflow. Local commissioning budgets cap volumes. No overflow mechanism if demand spikes. Working position is that paying for excess is cheaper than engineering caps. Central backstop not yet agreed.

Three-tier pricing structure (mobilisation + activity + volume floor). Activity tier measured on cost per valid result, not flat per-kit. Per-kit was rejected through the RFI and supplier forums. Subscription was rejected at strategic level. The three-tier shape gives suppliers a predictable floor while incentivising return rates on the activity tier. Final activity-unit position is A9 in the recommendation paper. Health economics modelling substantiates the activity-tier cost base.

Addressable market sizing. No quantified model exists. Working order-of-magnitude is one million tests and roughly £50m at scale. Without a hard number the options paper stays narrative.

Centralised payment. Working position is that payment flows run between commissioner and supplier directly. A centralised invoicing model has been flagged as a longer-term option but not designed.

6. Information governance

The IG artefact lifecycle. Ngozi (IG / DPO) runs it end-to-end: setting the lawful basis, approving the DPIA, executing DSAs and DPAs, signing off go-live, running the audit and refresh cycle. The other actors are consulted at specific points, but the IG lane is owned by IG. This lane runs alongside every configuration and every supplier admission.

Screening lens · pipes vs treatment plant
Every configuration is screened against two scenarios before the lawful basis question opens. Scenario 1 (pipes): NHSE is routing data between parties for direct care. The lawful basis already in place carries it; no new Direction is needed. Scenario 2 (treatment plant): NHSE is using the data for its own purpose (analytics, evaluation, performance). A new Direction is required. The HIV private beta runs as scenario 1, provided analytics stays bounded to evaluating whether the digital pathway works. Configurations move from scenario 1 to scenario 2 if the use of data expands; the test is re-run at every material change. This is a working screening lens reflecting senior IG steer; the formal IG / DPO position remains the authoritative sign-off.

How this lane works in steady state

The legal-direction document set. When a configuration sits outside the lawful basis the platform already carries, the route is a legal direction. The set is four artefacts: approved DPIA, technical specification, privacy notice, and Secretary of State letter. The DPIA is the core. The technical specification and privacy notice face the public and explain what is being done with the data. The Secretary of State letter is the formal sign-off, drafted in PTT, signed at the top of NHSE, and published online with the rest of the set.

The test for whether a legal direction is required. Five factors: what data, for what purpose, who is the controller, whether data is matched against other datasets (secondary uses), whether data is anonymous or pseudonymised. Run early in SHAPE, not at the end of CONTRACT. The widest plausible list of data controllers and data flows is asked for upfront, not bolted on later, because revising a direction mid-flight is painful.

Assumption discipline. When IG counterparts are slow to respond, the framework documents the working assumption, takes SLT sign-off, and embeds it in the programme board pack. The platform keeps moving while the formal IG channel is pressed. Assumptions are explicit, evidenced and traceable, so the gap between assumption and final decision is small when the answer lands.

Data controllers are a growing list, not a fixed set. Under Pathway 1 (LA-commissioned, patient-init) the controllers are NHSE for the foundation, the LA commissioner for the testing purpose, and the supplier as processor. Under Pathway 2 (acute-initiated) acute trusts join as controllers for their testing purpose. Under Pathway 3 (GP-initiated) GP practices join as controllers. The framework asks for the widest plausible list of controllers upfront when a new clinical area enters scope (the Wayfinder precedent), rather than returning for incremental directions each time a configuration adds a new controller type.

Legal-direction sequencing has a six-month lead time. A new or amended legal direction takes around six months end to end. The MI scope and data flows that the direction will cover have to be locked before the direction is requested. If the scope shifts after the direction is in flight, the request restarts, and the next available answer is two cycles away. The discipline is to converge the MI scope deliberately and only then trigger the direction. Re-asking PTT mid-flight burns goodwill as well as time.

Little and often. Shared outcomes. IG and delivery engage continuously rather than at gate moments, working to one shared view of what success looks like. The pattern is a steady cadence of short, focused IG conversations through the lifecycle, not a single heavyweight DPIA review at the end. It removes the cost of revisions, the cost of late surprises, and the cost of conflicting informal advice teams seek when the formal route is too slow. Standard inputs (data flow diagram, controllers map, lawful basis statement) have agreed quality expectations so the same evidence is not reproduced three different ways across three different reviews.

Open decisions

Data controller framework. Federated multi-controller model agreed in principle (NHSE for foundation, commissioner for testing purpose, supplier as processor). Joint-controller ruled out. Exact controller designation per configuration still being scoped. Lawful basis position (direct consent vs legal directions) unresolved at SLT level.

IAO appointment. NHSE policy requires the IAO to be a permanent NHSE employee. Programme heavily contractor-led; no eligible IAO yet identified. Critical blocker on go-live.

PTT scope is per clinical area. PTT teams are organised by clinical area, not by platform. The team that decides directions for the current clinical area is not the same team that decides directions for primary care, secondary care diagnostics, or screening outside the current condition. As the platform scales, it engages multiple PTT teams in parallel. The framework needs to be portable across them, with a clear handover pattern when a new clinical area first comes into scope.

National data infrastructure alignment. How HomeTest's IG framework dovetails with FDP (Federated Data Platform) and SPR (Single Patient Record) governance. Each carries its own lawful basis and controller designation; HomeTest aligns to them rather than creating parallel governance.

What the swimlanes expose

The framework administrator role is the hardest new capability. Whether NHSE self-administers or outsources, somebody has to run continuous supplier admission, performance management, pricing compliance, market health monitoring. That's a standing team, not a project function.

The centre's role is matchmaker, not contract counterparty. The centre runs onboarding, sets standards, builds integration, monitors market health. It does not sign local contracts on behalf of LAs, own billing, or operate logistics.

Systematisation is the work. Most of these processes need to be designed and built as standing capabilities, not as project-mode workarounds. The framework administrator role and the centre's standing onboarding, performance, and market-health functions are where this concentrates.

The financial lane is still the weakest part of the design. The PSA Active Surveillance Monitoring Episode tariff is now in design with a 26/27 to 28/29 glide-path, but the addressable market model, central funding for the platform itself, and overflow backstop remain unresolved.
