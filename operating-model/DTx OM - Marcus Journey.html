The supplier's real problem
Building a clinical-grade product is the easy bit. The market is the bit that's broken.
Marcus didn't lack regulatory clearance, evidence or a roadmap. He lacked a route in that wasn't bespoke per trust, per ICB, per procurement officer. Every NHS conversation started from zero. The framework is the bit that makes those conversations start from somewhere, with the work already done at the centre.

Four years in · the product is ready, the market isn't

Marcus has a clinical-grade digital therapeutic for COPD. Regulatory clean. Real-world evidence on adherence and admission reduction. What he doesn't have is a way in that scales.

I've got the evidence. I've got the clearance. I've got patients who've benefited from this product for years. What I haven't got is a way to put it in front of the NHS that doesn't start with a six-month conversation in a single trust.

Marcus
Where the product sits
CE-marked. MHRA-aligned. Two published RCTs and a real-world evidence base across six pilot sites. NICE relevance established. What he doesn't have: a national route in. Each new ICB conversation starts from zero, runs against a procurement template that wasn't designed for software, and ends in pilot purgatory: a small site, no follow-on, no clinical adoption signal, no commercial trajectory. The product is operating in a market that doesn't exist yet.
The pattern Marcus and every other DTx founder knows by heart
Pilot, evidence, pilot, evidence, pilot. Each new conversation re-relitigates clinical safety, IG, integration, contracting and reimbursement from scratch. Even when a trust says yes, it's a one-trust deal. Even when the evidence converts a clinician, it doesn't convert a system. The companies that survive aren't the ones with the best products; they're the ones with the most capital to wait out the procurement attrition.
👥
What his team is doing
Customer success across six pilot sites, each running on different commercial terms. A regulatory function maintaining MHRA and clinical safety case. A commercial team writing the same supplier due-diligence response template for the seventh, eighth and ninth ICB. None of this is scaling.

The framework appears · the door he didn't know was coming

A national framework for digital therapeutics. Three product overlays. One assurance bar. One contract architecture. One MI feed. Marcus reads the spec twice to make sure he's reading it right.

I've seen frameworks before. This one is different. Same admission bar, same contract, same MI feed across the whole estate. If it lands the way it's written, it's the route in we've been waiting for.

Marcus
What the model is doing underneath
A single national framework instrument carries three product overlays: HomeTest, HealthStore, HealthSync. For HealthStore suppliers, admission runs through the NICE EVA conditionally-recommended route into the National App Formulary, with DTAC, DCB0129 (manufacturer-side clinical safety) and the supplier admission gate sitting at Foundation level. Once admitted, Marcus is on a national catalogue available to every ICB on the framework. The work that used to happen per-trust happens once at the centre.
👥
What he's looking at on screen
Framework spec, admission requirements per overlay, contract architecture, MI requirements. Maximum pricing visible. Eligibility filters explained. The Pattern catalogue and Configuration layer that tell him which deployment shapes are admitted and which sit outside.

Self-assessment · whether the product actually fits the admission bar

The Self-Assessment Toolkit asks the four questions Marcus needs answered honestly before he commits a senior team to the admission process. Pathway fit. Clinical-safety case. IG profile. Evidence completeness.

It told me where the gaps were before I'd spent six months and four hundred grand finding out the hard way. Three of the four came back clean. The fourth was a known piece of work, costed and timetabled.

Marcus
What the model is doing underneath
The Supplier Self-Assessment Toolkit sits supplier-facing: the mirror image of the commissioner toolkit. Behind it is the Assurance Model coordinating NICE EVA, DTAC, DCB0129 and the supplier admission gate. The toolkit lets Marcus pre-qualify himself, see the evidence pack shape that admission will need, and decide whether to enter the formal admission process now, fix the one open gap first, or stand down. Assurance portability means the work he does is reusable across every ICB the framework reaches. Done once, consumed many times.
What admission used to look like
A 60-page DTAC response, a clinical-safety case rewritten from scratch every time, an IG questionnaire designed for a hospital IT system, and a procurement template that asked about uptime in seconds without ever asking about clinical outcomes. Today: a self-assessment that tells him whether to bother, a structured evidence pack, and a national admission process designed for software.
👥
Who's in the room with him
His head of clinical, his head of regulatory, his head of commercial. Plus the framework's supplier-engagement contact for the open question. Four people for an afternoon, not four months of consultant time.

NICE EVA admission · the conditional-recommendation route that changes the maths

Conditional recommendation under NICE EVA. Admission to the National App Formulary with the real-world evidence generation commitment baked in. Marcus is on the catalogue.

Conditional admission is the unlock. We don't have to wait for a randomised trial we'd never have funded. We have to keep generating real-world evidence, which is exactly what the product was always meant to do.

Marcus
What the model is doing underneath
The NICE EVA conditionally-recommended route is the admission mechanism that lets a clinical-grade DTx onto the National App Formulary on the basis of credible evidence plus a real-world evidence generation commitment, rather than waiting for the full RCT-grade economic case the traditional NICE route requires. For Marcus that turns a market that was previously closed for years into a market open now, with the explicit obligation that his product keeps generating the evidence that supports re-evaluation. The conditional admission is a contract: a route in, paired with a duty to keep proving the product is doing what it said it would do.
👥
What changes the day admission lands
The product is listed in the National App Formulary, visible to every ICB on the framework. Maximum pricing locked in. National terms applied. ICBs can call off without rebuilding the procurement. The MI obligations are explicit and the evidence-generation programme is the price of admission.

What Marcus's story shows

One operating model. One admission route.
A market that finally exists for the products it was always meant to serve.

For four years Marcus operated in a market that didn't exist yet, a UK NHS that wanted clinical-grade digital therapeutics and had no national mechanism for buying them. The operating model is the mechanism. NICE EVA conditional admission opens the door. The framework instrument carries the contract architecture. The Foundation layer carries the integration and the data spine. Suppliers like Marcus stop being capital-constrained pilot survivors and start being normal vendors with a normal route to scale. The market becomes a market.

For Marcus
A national market, not a series of deals.
One assurance pass. One contract instrument. One MI feed. Fourteen ICBs live in the time it used to take to negotiate one. The commercial team is doing service, not sales. The cost-of-NHS-sales line has fallen by nearly three quarters per ICB. The product is doing what it was always meant to do.

A clinical-grade DTx, prescribed normally.
Eleven thousand patients in the active cohort. NHS Login carries the identity. The NHS App is the front door. The DTx is just a prescription, like any other clinical option. The years Marcus didn't spend on per-trust procurement is years of patients who got the product faster.

A supplier base that can actually exist.
A market with a real admission route is a market that attracts companies. A supplier base with a real route to scale is a supplier base that survives long enough to keep improving the product. The evidence Marcus generates feeds the next NICE review, the next supplier's admission case and the next commissioner's confidence. The flywheel runs once.
