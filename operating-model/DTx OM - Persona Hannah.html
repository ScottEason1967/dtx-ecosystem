Hannah.

GP / primary care clinician · HomeTest iron deficiency anaemia
“My anaemia patients sit in front of me every six to twelve months for a review. The FBC and ferritin are the anchor of that conversation. If they can do the test at home and the result is in the record before they arrive, the consultation gets to be about what we do next, not about chasing bloods.”
Hannah is a GP in a primary-care practice that holds the long-term-condition register for her local population. Her clinical caseload threads through anaemia, women's health, hypertension, CVD risk, asthma and COPD. She is the clinician who initiates routine FBC and ferritin orders during her routine review consultations and acts on the results when they return to the GP record. She is Layla's GP on the HomeTest iron deficiency anaemia configuration.

What she does in the pathway

Hannah is the GP who initiates the FBC and ferritin order from her clinical system during a routine review consultation. She is also the clinician the result returns to: the result lands in the GP record automatically, with a flag if iron stores or haemoglobin are out of range. Where the result is in-range, the patient continues on routine monitoring. Where it is out of range, Hannah contacts the patient to discuss the next step: medication adjustment, escalation, referral on. The configuration is in design phase; Hannah's clinical role is well understood, the commissioning route around her is the open question.

What she needs from HomeTest

  • Ordering from inside her clinical system. The order needs to be available from the GP system she already uses, not a separate web tool. If she has to log into a parallel system, ordering rates collapse.
  • Result back in the GP record at the right point. The result has to land in the same patient record she already reviews, flagged in the same way as a lab-side FBC and ferritin, with the same colour-coding for out-of-range. The result needs to look like a routine blood test, not a new artefact.
  • A clear out-of-range pathway. When the result comes back high, the system needs to flag it to her at the right priority. She is the safety net for the patient on this pathway; missed escalation is the worst-case failure mode.
  • A confidence story she can pass to the patient. Layla and her peers will ask why their bloods are being done by post rather than in-surgery. Hannah needs a one-line answer that holds up: the test is the same test, the result is the same result, the lab is accredited, the only thing that has changed is the convenience.
  • Workload that nets out flat or better. Reviews still happen; ordering shifts from in-clinic phlebotomy to a HomeTest order during the previous consultation. If the net workload on her practice goes up, adoption stalls regardless of clinical merit.

Where Hannah sits relative to the other clinicians

Hannah is one of the named clinicians in the portfolio. Adam is the consultant urologist who initiates PSA-AS tests in an acute trust setting and interprets the result against a patient's monitoring trend. Ravi is the HealthStore prescriber who admits patients to a digital therapeutic. Hannah is the GP archetype who initiates iron studies during primary-care anaemia reviews. Same role; different specialty and care setting. Anita is the commissioner on Hannah's configuration: she buys the service, Hannah uses it.
